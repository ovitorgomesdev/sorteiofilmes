Accessibility

Definition: Designing products, devices, services, or environments so that people with disabilities can use them.

Contextual Example: Adding descriptive "alt text" to images on a website allows screen readers to describe the image content to visually impaired users.

Value: Ensures that digital products can be used by the widest possible audience, promoting inclusivity and reaching more users.

Affordance

Definition: The perceived properties of an object that suggest how it can be used. It's about how an object's design communicates its function.

Contextual Example: A button that is slightly raised and has a shadow visually "affords" being pressed or clicked. A scrollbar affords scrolling because it looks like it can be moved up and down.

Value: Guides users intuitively on how to interact with an interface, reducing confusion and the need for explicit instructions.

Agile

Definition: An iterative and incremental approach to project management and software development that delivers value in small, frequent cycles.

Contextual Example: A team developing a mobile app might release a new version with a few new features every two weeks (a "sprint"), gathering feedback constantly.

Value: Allows for flexibility, rapid adaptation to change, continuous feedback, and faster delivery of working software.

Back-end/Front-end

Definition: Front-end refers to the client-side of a website or application, what users see and interact with (UI). Back-end refers to the server-side, which handles data storage, logic, and communication with databases.

Contextual Example: When you fill out a form on a website, the form itself (buttons, text fields) is front-end. When you submit the form, the back-end processes your data and stores it.

Value: Understanding this distinction is crucial for knowing where design and development efforts are focused (e.g., UX/UI is primarily front-end, while database management is back-end).

Backlog

Definition: A prioritized list of features, bug fixes, technical tasks, or work items that a product development team needs to address for a product.

Contextual Example: Before starting a new feature, a product team reviews the product backlog to decide what to work on next, based on importance and urgency.

Value: Provides a clear roadmap for development, ensures that the most valuable work is done first, and helps in managing project scope.

Beta Testing

Definition: A stage of product development where a nearly complete version of a product is released to a limited group of external users for real-world testing before official launch.

Contextual Example: A software company invites a small group of users to try out their new mobile app for a month to identify any bugs or usability issues before its public release.

Value: Helps identify bugs, usability problems, and performance issues in a real-world environment that might have been missed during internal testing, leading to a more robust final product.

Breadcrumb

Definition: A secondary navigation aid that shows users their location within a website or application, typically as a horizontal list of links.

Contextual Example: On an e-commerce site, you might see "Home > Electronics > Laptops > Gaming Laptops" at the top of the page, indicating your current location.

Value: Improves website usability by helping users understand their current location in the site hierarchy and providing an easy way to navigate back to previous sections.

Bug

Definition: An error, flaw, or fault in a computer program or system that causes it to produce an incorrect or unexpected result, or to behave in unintended ways.

Contextual Example: A user clicks a "Submit" button on a form, but nothing happens. This indicates a bug in the code preventing the form submission.

Value: Identifying and fixing bugs is crucial for ensuring product stability, reliability, and a positive user experience. Unaddressed bugs can lead to user frustration and product abandonment.

Call-to-Action (CTA)

Definition: A prompt on a website or application that tells the user to take a specific, desired action, often in the form of a button or hyperlink.

Contextual Example: Buttons like "Sign Up Now", "Add to Cart", "Download Free Ebook", or "Learn More" are all examples of CTAs.

Value: Guides users towards conversion goals (e.g., making a purchase, signing up for a newsletter) and improves the effectiveness of marketing and user flows.

Card Sorting

Definition: A UX research technique used to understand how users categorize and organize information. Participants sort cards (representing website content or features) into groups that make sense to them.

Contextual Example: During website redesign, users might be given cards with terms like "Shipping Information," "Contact Us," "Order History," and "Product Categories," and asked to group them and label the groups.

Value: Helps in designing intuitive information architectures, navigation menus, and content structures that align with users' mental models, making content easier to find.

Grammar & Functional Language Toolkit üõ†Ô∏è

When you talk UX/UI, precision is key! Here's how to sound super smart when defining and explaining value:

  • Comparative Adjectives (for distinguishing concepts): Use these when you're comparing two awesome concepts!
    • "UX is broader than UI ‚Äì like a whole journey versus a single amazing view!"
    • "Accessibility is more important for inclusive design than just making things look pretty."
  • Superlative Adjectives: Use these when something is the absolute best or most significant among a group!
    • "Agile is often considered the most effective methodology for rapid development because it's so flexible."
    • "A clear CTA is the most crucial element for conversion; it's the big 'GO!' button!"
  • Phrases for Defining: Get ready to define like a dictionary!
    • "X is defined as..."
    • "X refers to..."
    • "X is essentially..."
  • Phrases for Explaining Value: Show off *why* these concepts matter!
    • "The value of X is that it enables... users to feel empowered."
    • "X helps to improve/reduce/ensure... a super smooth user journey."
    • "X is crucial because it leads to... happier users and successful products!"

Scenario: The Confusing Checkout üõí

You're tasked with improving the checkout process for an online store. Users are constantly abandoning their carts at the final step, complaining it's "too hard to use." What UX/UI concepts from this week would you focus on to fix this, and why?

Suggested Solution Focus:

  • Affordance: Ensure all clickable elements (buttons, links) *look* like they can be clicked. Is the "Place Order" button clearly a button?
  • Call-to-Action (CTA): Is the primary "Place Order" or "Complete Purchase" CTA highly visible, clear, and persuasive? Is there only one primary CTA on the page?
  • Breadcrumb: Is there a clear breadcrumb navigation (e.g., Cart > Shipping > Payment > Review) to show users where they are in the process and how to go back? This reduces confusion and "bug-like" feelings.
  • Beta Testing (for improvement): After implementing changes, conduct beta testing with real users to catch any remaining issues before a full launch.
  • Bug Fixing: Investigate if there are actual technical bugs causing forms to fail or pages to not load correctly, which directly impacts the user experience and leads to abandonment.

These concepts are crucial for creating a smooth, intuitive, and trustworthy checkout experience, ultimately reducing cart abandonment!

Job Interview Practice: Ace that Interview! üíº

Time to shine! Practice explaining UX/UI concepts like a true professional. This activity will help you articulate your understanding clearly and confidently, focusing on the value and practical application of each term.

Instructions:

  1. Pair up with a classmate and take turns being the "interviewer" and the "candidate."
  2. The interviewer picks a question from the list below and asks the candidate.
  3. The candidate should define the concept, explain its value, and ideally provide a great example.
  4. The interviewer gives constructive feedback on clarity, completeness, and how confident the candidate sounds!

Sample Interview Questions:

  • "Could you explain what Accessibility means in the context of web design and why it's super important?"
  • "How would you describe Affordance, and can you give an example of good vs. poor affordance in a digital product?"
  • "Our team uses an Agile methodology. What does Agile mean to you, and how does it benefit a design team in the real world?"
  • "What's the primary difference between Front-end and Back-end development, and how do they relate to UX/UI in your work?"
  • "Imagine a website has a bug that prevents users from submitting a form. How might this mess up the user experience, and what's the value of quickly squashing that bug?"
  • "Can you give an example of a compelling Call-to-Action (CTA) you've seen, and what made it so effective?"

Pro Tips for Answering:

  • Define Clearly: Start with a precise, punchy definition.
  • Explain Value/Impact: Articulate *why* the concept is a game-changer (its benefits, how it tackles problems).
  • Provide Awesome Examples: Use real-world or clever hypothetical examples to make your point shine!
  • Be Concise: Aim for clear, to-the-point answers ‚Äì like a mini-superpower explanation!
  • Use Functional Language: Practice using phrases like "X is defined as...", "The value of X is...", "X helps to...".
